Our story

We planned
the farm.
We didn't plan
the ravens.

A nearly 200-year-old house.
Far more optimism
than experience.

In 2023, we packed up four kids under the age of eight and set out to renovate a nearly 200-year-old house in the Annapolis Valley with far more optimism than experience.

We brought nine chickens with us from Ontario. That was the beginning. The goats came next, and the rest, as they say, is history.

Call it a hobby farm — or one of those hobbies that quietly becomes a full-time job with zero benefits and zero pay, aside from an unreasonable amount of poop and the benefit of fresh eggs.

The name

Two ravens were
waiting on moving day.
They haven't left.

They circled the driveway as we pulled in, as if they'd been expecting us. Not long after, we realized they'd been here long before us and had no intention of going anywhere.

Even now they circle above us several times a day, keeping watch, and happily accepting eggs when offered. We named the place after them. It felt right.

"We are raising our children to notice, to wonder, to adapt, and to work hard. We are thrilled to have them joining us on this crazy little adventure."
What we've learned

Three years in.

The animals don't care what's on the calendar

Feed them anyway. That rhythm has done more for this family than any routine we've tried to build on purpose.

Kids do better with real stakes

A sick goat. A late frost. An egg that didn't make it. The farm teaches things no curriculum does — and the kids are better for it.

Someone always needs something

And one of us always seems to find the mud or the poop. But it's home and we wouldn't change it for the world.
